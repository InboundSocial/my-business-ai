- **Format:** Annotated diagram labeling the 4 zones of a Facebook ad (5th zone — CTA — is implicit)

  - The "USB Mystery" ad (Ad 1 from image 001) used as the example
  - Four labeled zones with dotted lines pointing to specific ad elements:
    1. **🥷 Slippery Lead-in Copy** → points to first lines: "'THIS client getting hack feels illegal...'"
    2. **🚨 Pattern Interrupt Image** → points to the circular inset with orange object + red arrow
    3. **👀 Eyeball Grabbing Headline** → points to: "THIS Client Getting 'Hack' Feels Illegal"
    4. **🤔 Intriguing Link Description** → points to: "(Watch Carefully. Then Click)"
  - "Learn More" CTA button visible (the implicit 5th zone)
  - Social proof metrics: 41 reactions, 5 comments, 6 shares
- **Why it matters:**
  - This is the MASTER REFERENCE for ad structure — every ad should be evaluated against these 4+1 zones
  - The slippery lead-in is the MOST OVERLOOKED zone — changing just this has 5x'd ROAS in tests
  - Each zone has a separate psychological job: stop (image) → hook (headline) → pull (lead-in) → direct (link desc) → convert (CTA)

- **What to steal:**
  - Use this as a QA checklist: does every ad have all 5 zones engineered for curiosity?

  - The lead-in copy is above the image but read AFTER the image stops the scroll
  - Link descriptions should be instructions ("Watch Carefully. Then Click") not descriptions ("Visit our website")
  - Always "Learn More" — never "Shop Now" or "Buy Now" or "Sign Up"

## Pattern Summary

> Recurring techniques across the catalog. These are the universal principles — not templates.

### The Dual-Image Technique (001, 004, 005, 008)
Two images force the brain to comprehend two things simultaneously, which stops scrolling.
The secondary image (usually a circular inset) must do SEPARATE psychological work from
the primary. Primary = context. Inset = clue/proof/reaction. The viewer's brain creates
a third meaning by connecting them.

### Curiosity Gaps That Never Close (001, 003, 006, 008, 009)
The headline raises a question and NEVER answers it. The answer is behind the click.
"What is that object?" "What bombshell?" "Which suburb?" "What warning?" Specificity
about the STAKES ("will affect millions," "$500k month") without specificity about the
ANSWER.

### Red Annotations = "Leaked/Exposed" Signal (001, 004, 011)
Hand-drawn red circles and arrows mimic "someone marked this up to show you something
secret." It implies the image was annotated by a person with insider access, not designed
by a marketing team. Works on photos, screenshots, and documents.

### Content-Native Formats (002, 005, 008)
The best-performing ads look like content, not ads. Strip brand signals (logos, CTAs,
product shots) from the creative. Mimic the formats people already engage with: LadBible
posts, news broadcasts, UGC stories. The ad filter is visual — bypass it by not looking
like an ad.

### Tabloid Language Over Marketing Language (003, 005, 009, 010, 011)
"Infamous" not "well-known." "Drops bombshell" not "announces." "Exposed" not "revealed."
Tabloid language triggers emotional processing; marketing language triggers ad-filter
processing. Use single quotes around key emotional words for emphasis.

### The 5-Zone Framework (007)
Every Facebook/Instagram ad has 5 zones that each need curiosity engineering:
1. Slippery Lead-in Copy (most overlooked — 5x ROAS improvement potential)
2. Pattern Interrupt Image (the creative)
3. Eyeball Grabbing Headline
4. Intriguing Link Description ("Watch Carefully. Then Click" not "Visit our website")
5. Learn More CTA (always — never Buy Now)

### Specificity = Believability (001, 002, 006)
"90-480 qualified leads/month" not "more leads." "$500k sales month" not "great results."
"Secret VIC suburb" not "hidden gem." Specific numbers, locations, timeframes, and
names make claims concrete and trustworthy.
