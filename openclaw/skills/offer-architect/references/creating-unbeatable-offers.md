## Everything Is An Offer
Even if there is no financial exchange.

## A Product Or Service Is Only A Component of the Offer

## Know The Prospect
You must know who you are creating the offer for so that you can cut through the noise.

- What problem do they desperately want resolved?
- Why do they want to solve this problem now?
- What do they want in a solution?
- What don’t they want in a solution?
- What other solutions have they been exposed to?
- Which ones are most popular?
- How do they feel about the problem? The available solutions?
- What beliefs do they have which you need to understand to best resonate and align with them?

## Know The Competitors
The point of analyzing competitors is to know what is already being offered.

- What problem are they solving?
- What promise are they making?
- What are the attributes of their solution?
- What sort of language are they using?
- What is their **unique mechanism** (if any)—how their product or service works to deliver the solution?
- What is their proposition?

## The New Opportunity
You do not want to create an *improvement offer* in order to set yourself apart.

You must create a **whole new opportunity**.

### What “New Opportunity” Means
A new opportunity is something entirely new and different that presents a chance for a significant change or breakthrough. It typically involves:

- **Unique concept:** introducing something that the target market has not seen before.
- **New pathways:** offering a new way to achieve a desired outcome, often through innovative products or services.
- **Higher perceived value:** often perceived as more valuable because it promises a complete transformation rather than incremental improvements.
- **Untapped market:** it may target a market that hasn’t been addressed or create a new market altogether.

### Examples of New Opportunities
1. **iPhone:** when first introduced, it wasn’t just an improvement over existing phones; it redefined what a phone could be.
2. **Airbnb:** created a new way for people to find lodging, different from traditional hotels and travel accommodation.
3. **Tesla:** introduced electric cars as a viable and attractive alternative to gasoline-powered vehicles, creating a new market for high-performance electric vehicles.
4. **Uber:** revolutionized transportation by offering a ride-hailing service that connects drivers with passengers through a mobile app, fundamentally changing urban transportation.
5. **Netflix:** transitioned from a DVD rental service to a streaming platform, offering unlimited access to movies and TV shows online, disrupting traditional cable TV and rental markets.
6. **Bitcoin:** introduced a new form of digital currency that operates on a decentralized ledger called blockchain, creating the cryptocurrency market and revolutionizing digital transactions.

## Components of a Winning Offer
> Your offer becomes irresistible when it’s stacked with **benefits**, not deliverables.

## The Attractive Solution
An attractive solution:

- Solves a significant problem or unfulfilled desire
- Produces a big, desirable result
- Is viewed as quick and easy to implement  
  *This is not about saying “it’s easy”; it’s about engineering the solution to make it easy for the prospect.*
- Is viewed as producing fast results  
  - How fast will I see the transformation?
  - How quickly does it begin working?
  - When will I begin seeing results?  
  *Even if your solution takes time, you can still demonstrate an aspect of speed in another area.*
- Is viewed as new and different
- Is seen as superior to other options  
  - What is a statement you can claim and back up with proof?
- Can be positioned/framed as **DFY** (Done-For-You)
- Gives the prospect confidence in outcome (comes from the above)

## Unbeatable Proposition
> A superior choice over competitors, irresistible outcomes (benefits), and assured peace of mind from clear-cut terms and risk reversals.

### Deliverables
Deliverables are what they get: the features, why they get it, and the benefits they will enjoy.

Guidelines:
- Each deliverable should address a **different** benefit, overcome a different concern, or resolve a different problem.
- If your deliverables address the same benefit or challenge, it conveys that one or all of them are inferior—hence why you felt you needed additional deliverables.
- Each deliverable should have a **benefit-driven** name/title.

Examples of deliverable types:
- Videos
- Worksheets
- Cheat sheets
- Community
- Coaching
- Experiences
- Outcomes
- Etc.

## Benefits
There are **3 types of benefits**:
1. **Functional** — what does it do for the prospect?
2. **Experiential** — paint a picture; bring the functional to life.
3. **Emotional** — convey how the experiential benefit will make them feel.

Check your benefit stack:
- Did you show how the prospect’s desires and deep emotional needs can be met by your product better, faster, easier than anything else?
- Did you turn your benefits into the fulfillment of core desires?
- Make the acquisition of core desires shine through the product itself.
- Did you focus on the results/improvements the offer gives the prospect?

### D.F.W. + 3 Benefits Template
This follows the **Deliverable (D)**, **Feature (F)**, **Why (W)**, plus **3 benefits** structure.

**Example:**
You get a year-long subscription to **Financial Freedom**, featuring real-time financial tracking and personalized budgeting tools to ensure you manage your money more efficiently. So you can finally take control of your finances without the guesswork. From the moment you start using **Financial Freedom**, you'll feel an immediate sense of organization and clarity over your financial situation, empowering you to make smarter spending decisions. And you’ll feel reassured and confident, knowing you’re paving the way toward financial freedom—a goal you’ve always aimed for, and you deserve!


## Limiting Beliefs
When it comes to selling your offers, you’ll be faced with three limiting beliefs people usually have:

- **Internal beliefs** — makes them think for some reason the product/service is not right for them.
- **External beliefs** — something external is going to hold them back.
- **Vehicle beliefs** — the vehicle/delivery mechanism may not work for them or be right for them.

### Example Scenario: Homeowner Needs a New Furnace

#### Internal Belief
- **Example:** “I don’t know enough about furnaces to make sure the contractor is doing a good job.”
- **Explanation:** The homeowner doubts their own knowledge and ability to evaluate the work being done, fearing they might not get a quality installation.

#### External Belief
- **Example:** “I don’t have the budget to hire a professional contractor for the furnace installation.”
- **Explanation:** The homeowner believes financial constraints are a barrier to hiring a professional, thinking the service will be too expensive.

#### Vehicle Belief
- **Example:** “Hiring a contractor to install the furnace might not solve my heating issues because they could use low-quality parts or cut corners.”
- **Explanation:** The homeowner doubts the effectiveness and integrity of the contractor’s service, worrying that the installation might not be reliable or long-lasting.

### Overcoming These Beliefs
1. **Internal beliefs**
   - **Strategy:** Provide educational materials, offer consultations, and ensure transparent communication throughout the project.
   - **Message:** “You don’t need to be an expert on furnaces to get a quality installation. Our team will guide you through every step and answer any questions you have. We believe in clear, transparent communication to ensure your peace of mind.”

2. **External beliefs**
   - **Strategy:** Offer competitive pricing, financing options, and highlight the cost-effectiveness and long-term savings of a new furnace.
   - **Message:** “Installing a new furnace is an investment in your home’s comfort and efficiency. We offer competitive pricing and flexible financing options to make it affordable. Plus, a new, energy-efficient furnace can save you money on your heating bills.”

3. **Vehicle beliefs**
   - **Strategy:** Highlight the contractor’s qualifications, use of high-quality materials, and provide warranties and customer testimonials.
   - **Message:** “Our contractors are highly skilled and experienced in furnace installations. We use only high-quality parts and offer a satisfaction guarantee. Hear from our satisfied customers who have experienced reliable and efficient heating with their new furnaces.”

## Premiums & Bonuses
Premiums/bonuses are meant to not only stack the value and make your products and services more valuable, but also to address and knock down limiting beliefs.

- Use these to fill product gaps and remove “excuses” for not buying.
- Give a reason why you’re including them.
- Must include: features, why, and functional/experiential/emotional benefits.
- Sell these just as hard as the main product. *(These should be as valuable as the main product.)*
- Multiple is better than single (but not too many).

## Price
> By far the biggest mover is the **soft offer**.

### Types of Offers
- **Hard offer** (pay now)
- **Soft offer** (pay later)

### Variations (Could Be for Either Hard or Soft)
- Instalment offers (payment plans)
- Negative option offer (continuity)

### Offer Positioning
- Charter offer — early one-time discount
- Exclusive offer — offer for certain folks only
- Limited offer — scarcity-driven offer
- Limited-time offer — deadline-driven offer
- One-time offer — offer only seen once
- Qualified offer — application required
- Combination offer — combining more than two above

## Payment Terms
Payment terms will significantly move the needle more than manipulating the price. Before you think about lowering your prices, think about different ways to utilize payment terms to maximize the value of a transaction.

Examples of how you might change payment terms include switching **how** and **when** the payment is made.

For instance, a soft offer (no payment upfront) will beat a hard offer every time.

The problem is that many people don’t have adequate cash flow to be able to operate on soft offers. But now, thanks to upsell technology, soft offers can become hugely scalable by implementing additional steps in the sales funnel to try and recoup some of the initial acquisition cost.

## Risk Reversals & Guarantees
There’s always a risk associated with anything. Even free offers carry risk (loss of time, inconvenience, hassle, etc.).

### Types of Risk
- Risk of financial loss
- Risk of loss of status
- Risk of loss of time
- Risk of inconvenience
- Risk of hassle or intimidation
- Risk of unsatisfactory results
- Risk of disappointment and frustration

## Types of Risk Reversals
There are two types of risk reversals:
1. **Conditional**
2. **Unconditional**

## Conditional Guarantee
A conditional guarantee provides a refund or replacement only if certain conditions are met. The conditions are specified upfront and can include time limits, usage restrictions, or specific criteria the product/service must meet.

**Key features:**
- Specific conditions (e.g., within 30 days, product must be returned in original condition)
- Time limits (a specific time frame within which the guarantee is valid)
- Criteria (specific criteria or performance metrics the product/service must meet)

**Example:**  
“If your new furnace fails to keep your home at the desired temperature within the first 90 days, we will repair it free of charge or provide a full refund.”

## Unconditional Guarantee
An unconditional guarantee provides a refund or replacement with no strings attached. The customer can return the product or cancel the service for any reason within a specified period, with no conditions or questions asked.

**Key features:**
- No conditions (applies regardless of the reason for dissatisfaction)
- Broad coverage (covers a wide range of situations, making it more reassuring)
- Simplicity (easy to understand and use)

**Example:**  
“If you are not completely satisfied with your new furnace installation within the first 90 days, we will give you a full refund, no questions asked.”

## Weighing The Options

| | **Unconditional** | **Conditional** |
|---|---|---|
| **Advantage** | Highly attractive to customers, builds trust and confidence in the product/service, can lead to increased sales. | Helps protect the business from unreasonable returns, sets clear expectations for customers, and can be tailored to specific business needs. |
| **Disadvantage** | Higher risk for the business due to potential for more returns/cancellations; may attract customers who are not genuinely interested. | May be seen as less customer-friendly due to conditions, which can discourage potential buyers. |

## Types of Guarantees
Examples of different ways you can use guarantees:
- Performance-based
- Double your money back
- Time-based
- Lifetime
- Keep the premiums
- Etc.

> Use the biggest and boldest guarantee possible—a risk reversal that your competitors think is crazy. You want your offer to be as close to risk-free as possible.

## Presenting Your Offer
The 4 parts of an offer presentation:
1. Product presentation
2. Price presentation
3. Risk-reversal presentation
4. Call to action presentation

## The Product Presentation

### The Bridge (from Marketing to Offer)
> By now, you see how and why the **[UNIQUE MECHANISM]** is so effective at **[PRIMARY PROMISE]**.  
> And you understand what makes it so powerful for **[VARIATION OF PRIMARY PROMISE]**.  
> And that’s why today I want to give you the opportunity to put it to work for you with a unique product/service called **[PRODUCT NAME]**.  
> Let me tell you what it will do for you, how quickly it will work, and everything you get today.

Now present your product/offer (follow the 5 steps for **each deliverable**):

1. Deliverable  
2. Feature  
3. Why  
4. Benefit(s)  
5. Proof  

**Majority of the focus should be on the benefit.**

*If you have not already done so, you can go through the Unique Mechanism training (referenced in the original deck).*

## The Price Presentation
Include:
- The regular price
- The discount percentage
- The rationale for the discount
- The offer price
- The savings percentage
- The savings amount
- Price trivialization (e.g., “the price of a cup of coffee a day”)

If presenting a payment plan along with a paid-in-full option:
- Present the **payment plan first**
- Follow with the **paid-in-full** option with additional savings as a reward

**NO:** $1,000 or four instalments of $299  
**YES:** $299 today, then three monthly instalments of $299, **or save $196** with one payment today of only $1,000.

Notes:
- The first looks like they are being penalized for opting for the payment plan, whereas the second looks like they are being rewarded for paying in full (penalty vs. reward).
- Notice the use of **instalment** vs. payments.
- Try to avoid words associated with cost/work/pain.

## The Risk Reversal Presentation
> Always state your benefits in the affirmative.

**NO:** If you’re unhappy or don’t get results, you get your money back.  
**YES:** You’re either thrilled and blown away by the results, or you get your money back.

*The first introduces the idea of being unhappy or not getting results (creating doubt). The second introduces a win-win: either you’re thrilled, **or** you get your money back.*
