# Money Models Reference

> Source: Alex Hormozi, $100M Money Models (2025)
> Core thesis: A Money Model is a deliberate sequence of offers — what you offer, when, and how —
> designed so one customer pays for acquiring at least two more in under 30 days.

---

## The Three Stages

| Stage | Goal | Offer Types |
|-------|------|-------------|
| I: Get Cash | Acquire customers for less | Attraction Offers |
| II: Get More Cash | Make more from each customer, faster | Upsell & Downsell Offers |
| III: Get the Most Cash | Maximize total lifetime spend | Continuity Offers |

---

## Stage I: Attraction Offers

Purpose: Get attention, fill the pipeline, lower effective customer acquisition cost.

### 1. Giveaways

Give something away to generate leads at scale.

- Framework: Run a contest or giveaway that attracts your exact ICP
- Contractor example: "Oldest HVAC Unit Contest" — generated 30 replacement leads from one giveaway
- Contractor example: "Oldest Roof in Town" contest, donate a roof to a veteran
- Key: the giveaway must attract people who need your service, not freebie seekers

### 2. Decoy Offers

Low-cost or free entry point that opens the door for the real sale.

- Framework: Offer a small service at below-market price to get in the door
- Contractor example: $29 service call instead of $99
- Contractor example: Free leak detection, free inspection
- Key: the decoy must lead naturally to the full-price service

### 3. Buy X, Get Y Free

Classic bundling that increases perceived value.

- Framework: Pair a paid service with a complementary free add-on
- Contractor example: Buy exterior window cleaning, get interior free
- Contractor example: Buy a new furnace, get a humidifier free
- Key: the free item should have high perceived value but low marginal cost to you

### 4. Pay Less Now, Pay More Later

Encourage early commitments with discounts.

- Framework: Offer a discount for prepayment or early booking
- Contractor example: Prepay for spring + fall gutter cleanings at a discount in January
- Contractor example: Discounted annual lawn packages if customers prepay in winter
- Key: boosts cash flow in slow seasons and locks in work for the year

## Stage II: Upsell Offers

Purpose: Maximize revenue from each customer interaction. The only thing worse than a $100 customer saying no to a $1,000 offer is a $1,000 customer saying yes to a $100 offer.

### 5. Classic Upsells

"You can't have this without that."

- Framework: Pair the primary service with a necessary or protective add-on
- Contractor example: Surge protector with an AC unit, safety tank with a water heater
- Key: the upsell should feel like responsible advice, not a sales push

### 6. Menu Upsells (Good, Better, Best)

Guide customers to self-select into higher tiers.

- Framework: Present 3 options — basic, standard, premium
- Contractor example: Basic tune-up vs. full maintenance vs. comprehensive service agreement
- Key: most customers pick the middle option; the premium option makes the middle feel reasonable

### 7. Anchor Upsells

Present the premium option first so everything else feels like a deal.

- Framework: Show the $20K system first, then the $12K option feels like a steal
- Contractor example: Present the top-tier HVAC system, then show the mid-range
- Key: the anchor reframes the customer's price reference point

### 8. Rollover Upsells

Credit previous purchases toward bigger-ticket sales.

- Framework: Apply past spend toward a larger purchase, reframing it as "already invested"
- Contractor example: Apply $750 in HVAC maintenance plan payments toward a new system
- Contractor example: Credit $450 gutter cleaning toward full gutter guard installation
- Key: this reframes the spend as money already invested, accelerating big-ticket conversions

## Stage II (cont.): Downsell Offers

Purpose: Save the sale when the customer hesitates. Catch the ones who would otherwise walk.

### 9. Payment Plans

Offer financing instead of losing the job to sticker shock.

- Framework: Break the total into monthly payments; make the monthly number the focus
- Contractor example: "$12,000 system" becomes "$199/month for 60 months"
- Key: remove the barrier of lump-sum payment; the monthly cost should feel manageable

### 10. Trial with Penalty

Free first service, but it costs more if they don't commit upfront.

- Framework: Offer a free or discounted first service; ongoing service is more expensive unless they sign up now
- Contractor example: Free first mow, but ongoing service is higher rate unless they commit to seasonal plan
- Key: creates urgency and rewards commitment

### 11. Feature Downsells

Offer a stripped-down version of the service.

- Framework: Remove features to hit a lower price point rather than losing the sale entirely
- Contractor example: Exterior-only window cleaning instead of full interior + exterior
- Key: you still win the customer and can upsell later

## Stage III: Continuity Offers

Purpose: Create recurring revenue and maximize lifetime value. This is where most home service businesses leave the most money on the table.

### 12. Bonus Continuity

Add a bonus for signing up for recurring service.

- Framework: Give a free add-on when they commit to ongoing service
- Contractor example: Sign up for annual maintenance, get a free thermostat
- Contractor example: Enroll in gutter cleaning plan, get free downspout cleaning
- Key: the bonus should feel like a genuine gift, not a throwaway

**Founder adaptation:** _(add your take here)_

### 13. Discount Continuity

Discount early periods to get them into the recurring relationship.

- Framework: Reduce the cost of initial periods to lower the barrier to commitment
- Contractor example: Join now, get 3 months free
- Contractor example: First seasonal service at 50% off
- Key: the discount period must be short enough that LTV math still works

### 14. Waived Fees for Commitment

Remove an upfront cost in exchange for a longer commitment.

- Framework: Waive setup/inspection fees if they sign a multi-period agreement
- Contractor example: Waive the initial inspection fee if they sign up for a 2-year service plan
- Key: the waived fee should feel meaningful; the commitment length should match the service cadence

## Cross-Cutting Concepts

### Win Your Money Back

- Framework: Structure the offer so the customer's risk is eliminated — if the service doesn't deliver the promised outcome, they get their money back or credit toward a fix
- Use when: high-ticket services where sticker shock is the main objection
- Key: only works when you're confident in your delivery

### The Core Math

The goal of a Money Model is:
- **Revenue from one customer** covers the cost of acquiring **at least two more** customers
- This happens in **under 30 days**
- When this math works, you're "printing money" — growth becomes self-funding

### Sequencing Matters

A Money Model isn't picking one offer type. It's the deliberate sequence:
1. Attraction Offer gets them in the door
2. Upsell/Downsell maximizes the first transaction
3. Continuity Offer locks in recurring revenue
4. The combined revenue from steps 1-3 funds acquiring the next customers

### Buyback Rate

Source: Dan Martell, Buy Back Your Time

- Framework: Annual income ÷ 2,000 hours ÷ 4 = max hourly rate to delegate
- Example: $80,000 ÷ 2,000 = $40/hr ÷ 4 = $10/hr — you can pay up to $10/hr to delegate tasks and still come out ahead
- Use when: deciding what to delegate vs. do yourself; justifying hiring decisions
- Key: anything you're doing that could be done by someone at your buyback rate or below, you should delegate immediately
